The American beaver

The American Beaver, or more commonly known inside of north America simply as “the beaver”, is a large member of the rodent family. In fact, with a body of two to three feet in length, it is the second biggest of the rodents-second only to the monstrous capibara.

The most notable thing about the beaver is its large black paddle-like tail. If you look at it closely you can see it is covered with large scales, like a dragon or a dinosaur! Beavers have four short little legs and black feet with 5 little digits on each paw. Their front paws are more dextrous and are primarily used for things like digging, folding leaves while eating, and spinning off little twigs while gnawing at bark. Their back paws, aside from their first and second toes, are all webbed like little flippers to help with swimming.

On average their fur is brown in color. Actually, their fur consists of two different types and lengths: the long coarse outer hairs, and the short fine inner hairs. Underneath those layers of fur (and a layer of skin) is a thick layer of fat, to insulate the beaver in a cold environment (perhaps also useful for cold water settings).

Beavers have learned to coat their fur with castoreum-an oily substance secreted from “castor glands” located near the cloaca-to make it waterproof! This is important because beavers are semiaquatic-which means they spend a significant part of their time under or in the water. Their eyes are covered with a nictitating membrane-which is basically a coating that allows the beaver to see well under the water, it's as if they can see through their eyelids! Another cool aquatic trait of the beaver is that their nostrils and ears seal up when they go under the water; They can also close their lips behind the front teeth, which allows them to gnaw at things under the water, how neat! Beavers can hold their breath underwater for up to 15 minutes at a time. This is beneficial to their environment since beavers usually live in ponds, lakes, rivers, and streams across most of the USA (exception of the states that are mostly desert).

The beaver's home is referred to as a lodge. It is made of sticks and mud, often having access from under the water. Beavers are one of the few animals that are actively capable of messing up an entire eco system. They are great at making dams, so restricting the flow of water is a simple task for a beaver; they can even redistribute the flow or create a whole new body of water!

Their teeth are constantly and infinitely growing, so they are always gnawing at bark to stop them from growing too long (Perhaps this is why they frequently build dams, to make use of their labor!).

Their species became near extinct because of beaver fur being such a good resource for fur hats, as well as the different chemicals they secrete being great additives in perfumes etc. Their real saving grace was the sudden change in fashion trends.

The beaver is thought to have gotten to North America across the Bering Straight-which is basically a straight across the pacific and arctic oceans between Russia and USA. The first ever record of a beaver was found in Germany 10-12 million years ago. The first finding in North America however was 7 million years ago, of two beaver teeth, found near Dayvile, Oregon. The beaver is the official state mammal of Oregon and New York, and is also an official symbol of the National Wildlife of Canada.
